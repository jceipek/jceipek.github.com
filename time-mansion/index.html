Time Mansion

Take Back the Future
Game Design Document
Work in Progress - last updated 11/27/2013
The moment you enter the room, you know that something has gone wrong. Carlos is splayed out on the floor, eyes wide and unblinking, his right arm twisted at an impossible angle. You were sure that Christopher murdered Carlos in the other timeline, but you stopped him. Unless... Before the flash of realization passes, you're already leaping back through time to prevent the crime. If Beatrice hadn't spoken to Christopher and Katherine before the attack, none of this would have happened.

Vision

Imagine a game that changes based on how you play it. A game that tells a story in which everything that happens makes perfect sense because of what you've done. The events in the story are not predetermined; they evolve as you travel back and forth in time in order to change the ultimate course of events. You have free reign to change what has happened by interacting with characters that have motivations and behaviors inspired by classic Agatha Christie mystery fiction -- the kind of fiction that features guests trapped in a mansion, having sordid affairs, and murdering one another over petty squabbles.

You play as a time traveler thrust into this mess, gradually discovering how to shape events so that they play out in your favor. Maybe you decide to play the hero, saving lives by preventing characters from meeting under unfortunate circumstances. Perhaps you thrive on chaos and want to see if you can make all of the mansion's inhabitants attack one another.

It's all up to you.

Time mansion is a point-and-click puzzle adventure game that puts you in control of the story. No longer is the outcome a linear slog throug pre-scripted sequence designed by writers hundreds of miles away for "all audiences." Instead, each short playthrough is unique and won't have been experienced before by you or anyone else in the world.

Motivation

Most games treat gameplay and stories as distinct and opposing forces. most games melded with stories tend to be Frankenstein monsters. Players tend to either skip the story or skip the game. - A Theory of Fun for Game Design pg 128Most long-form video games can only offer the player a limited amount of agency over the story due to combinatorial explosion. With every new decision that the player can make, the game's developers must script two or more possible outcomes for the duration of the choice's impact. [...] The amount of paths necessary increases exponentially with the player's agency, and the workload for developers quickly rises from significant to completely untenable.- Quicksilver: Infinite Story: Procedurally Generated Episodic Narratives for Gameplay Solving puzzles like this one is the core gameplay in adventure games like The Longest Journey, but the narrative is primarily conveyed through conversations and video cutscenes.The Moonlighters provides the illusion of player agency using narrative listening, a technique that produces isolated story changes in response to player choices without drastically changing an otherwise linear narrative. A common approach is gradually revealing sections of a story upon completion of gameplay challenges.

Furthermore, most games have primarily linear narratives that fail to change based on what players do within the game. Linear stories are better suited to media like film and literature, since the distinguishing characteristic of games is interactivity. Time Mansion combines the video game as a box of possibilities style of storytelling with the context of a dynamic story system. It allows you, as the player, to explore long-term consequence chains and empowers you to create a time travel meta-narrative more compelling than a dynamic story system could ever create on its own.

Gameplay

Time Mansion is a mechanically simple game that plays like a first person adventure in the style of Myst. The mechanics consist of

  • Moving from room to room
  • Interacting with characters
  • Going back in time

Mechanics

Movement in Time Mansion must be extremely simple and intuitive. The core of the game is uncovering and manipulating chains of causality -- there is no place for first-person-shooter style movement controls. You will be able to see one room at a time, from one perspective. Tapping on one of the doors will instantly transport you to the next room. You will not have a visible avatar, so you will be able to envision yourself however you choose and you won't experience the classic adventure game disconnect of waiting for your avatar to catch up to your actions.

Interacting is the least specified aspect of the game and will need to be playtested thoroughly. A potential player-character interaction that may be successful is temporarily taking control of a computer-controlled character in order to make them behave uncharacteristically. This could be used to delay characters moving to other rooms in the mansion or to manipulate the emotional state and relationships of other characters.

Time travel will likely consist of clicking a single button to jump back to the beginning of the story. For the sake of simplicity and player experimentation, actions in other timelines will be erased as if they never happened. If this form of time travel proves too frustrating to players because of lost progress, it may be necessary to introduce a reverse time slider as an alternative to a single button.

End Conditions

A session of Time Mansion will end when all of the characters are dead or when it becomes time for the guests to leave. This artificial time limit will be chosen to ensure that the story being told remains compelling. At the same time, it increases the potential for player-driven goals such as "keep all the guests alive until the party ends."

Storytelling and AI

Time Mansion's unique approach to storytelling ensures that every action you take as a player directly affects the story. Something as simple as delaying a character from leaving a room can have far-reaching consequences in the life, death, and relationships of other individuals in the game.

Elegant rules allow players to focus on the experience of play rather than on the logic of the rules. Designing meaningful play involves building discernible and integrated relationships between action and outcome into all levels of the rules of a game.- Rules of Play: Game Design Fundamentals pg 139 Because of the time travel mechanic, the story needs to be completely deterministic: unless you intervene, a given story should always have the same outcome no matter how often you travel back in time. Without this property, you will come to suspect that your actions are meaningless. In order for a game to be fun to play, it needs to empower the player.

The heart of Time Mansion is the story simulator: a system that determines the behavior of a set of characters with initial relationships, personalities, emotions, and locations within a world over time.

Simulating Interactions

Characters in Time Mansion have fixed traits and fluid states that define their behavior.

In the simplest form of simulation, the traits simply specify their gender preferences for amorous relationships:

  • is (gender)
  • attracted to (gender(s))

Character states are more complex, including

  • alive? (true or false)
  • location (a room in the mansion)
  • path (a list of intended destinations)
  • emotion (happy, angry, sad, or guilty)
  • relationships (a set of connections with every other character specifying if they are friends, foes, or lovers)

When two characters meet in the same room, their states change based on their traits and current states as the result of an action. Consequently, Time Mansion can provide real-time character description and development. Character description and development is either ignored or done in cut-scenes (since this is too hard to implement in game form).- Games Telling Stories: A Brief Note on Games and Narratives in The New Media and Cybercultures Anthology In the story simulator prototype, characters can engage in the actions

  • murder
  • insult
  • complain
  • flirt
  • compliment
  • suffer guilt
  • plan retribution

These actions are highly contextual. For example, in the prototype, character A will only murder character B if they are both alive, there are no other individuals in the room, and they are enemies.

One of the biggest challenges in creating Time Mansion will be balancing the need to make them complex enough to create compelling stories and simple/obvious enough for players to predict.

Wordless Stories

When we think of stories, we usually think of explicit narratives. In novels, they are conveyed purely through words. In films, narratives are traditionally expressed through a combination of moving images, music, and verbal dialog.

However, words are not essential to storytelling. Consider the short story Luxo Jr. created by Pixar:

It conveys a simple narrative without any spoken dialog; the images and sounds tell the story.

Interpretive dance is a medium that is dedicated to conveying narrative without words. Wordless stories can be just as powerful as stories that use elegant language (something that is conspicuously missing from many game stories today). They have the additional benefit that they are more universal between cultures.

One of the early inspirations for Time Mansion was Punchdrunk's Sleep No More, a post-modern theatrical performance featuring interpretive dance and a repeating story that invited the audience to explore it from different perspectives.

Computers are notoriously bad at forming compelling sentences. Well-written dialog requires significant up-front work by writers and almost inevitably leads to strictly or largely linear stories. Games with dynamic stories like Quicksilver: Infinite Story usually need even more content than linear games do because they need to combine lists of situations and entities to generate passable dialog.

Time Mansion's stories will largely avoid this content problem; the stories will be told using simple body language and iconic imagery. The main content challenge in the game will be the database of contextual actions required by the story simulator.

Aesthetics

Time Mansion's theme of intrigue in a sealed manor will be heavily reflected in its art direction.

Visuals

The game will feature a layered, painterly art style inspired by David Hellman's A Lesson Is Learned. Its palette and visual style will be drawn from opulent 1930s decor as portrayed in films like Gosford Park, with an emphasis on warm, vivid reds, golds, and browns set against blue-tinted blacks, greys, and whites.

Music

Time Mansion will feature a dynamic soundtrack that changes based on player-character and character-character interactions. For example, the soundtrack will become more aggressive when characters insult one another.

Business Concerns

Target Audience

According to Women and Games: Technologies of the Gendered Self in The New Media and Cybercultures Anthology, there are three categories of women gamers:
  • Power Gamer - enjoys competition and creating powerful, intelligent avatars
  • Moderate Gamer - seeks to control the environment and to win via careful thought
  • Non-gamer - considers games to be an asocial, solitary activity for the interpersonally inept but views self as interpersonally competent

Time Mansion is designed to appeal to wide audiences (male and female) and ideally will not require any prior gameplay experience because of its simple mechanics, point-and-click interface, and emphasis on social interactions over fast-paced action and hand-eye coordination.

It's initial audience will primarily include fans of point-and-click adventure games and mystery fiction.

Because the game's themes include murder and romance, its target demographic is players 13 and up.

Target Platforms

Time Mansion doesn't require a platform with a powerful processor or graphics card. It is best-suited to platforms that are associated with with medium or large screens, two dimensional input methods, and short playsessions. It will initially be developed for mobile tablets (iOS and Android) and low-end computers (Windows, Mac OS X, and Linux).
